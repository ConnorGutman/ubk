Urban
Beekeeping?

Bees are crucial to agricultural production, but in recent years have been undergoing colony collapse disorder (CCD) due to habitat loss, climate change, and an array of other issues. Thankfully, many cities are discovering the benefits of beekeeping towards combatting declining bee populations, helping boost local agriculture, and giving birth to new local businesses.

Key Issues:

  • 90% of the Earthâ€™s trees and plants depend on pollinators for the continuation of their species.
  • Honey bee populations within North America are declining at an annual rate of over 44%.
  • Habitat loss due to road construction, land development, and agricultural development is a leading factor contributing to CCD.
  • The recent rise of parasites and diseases which target bees threaten pollinators in the wild.

Urban Strengths:

  • Urban bees receive warmer temperatures and a more plant life variety for pollination, have better protection from pesticides, and often are more productive.
  • Populations can be well maintained and protected through active oversight and care.
  • Creates a surplus of locally produced honey, wax, and other useful products for surrounding communities.
  • Urban Beekeeping helps build and promote a relationship between cities and their surrounding nature.

Where's all the buzz?

Until recently, most cities across the United States have banned or restricted urban beekeeping. Due to CCD though, several major cities sucuh as New York, Los Angeles, and Washington D.C. have lifted those restrictions. As a result, cities all over are launching exciting efforts to save the bees.

How do
I become a
beekeeper?

As with any new career or hobby, urban beekeeping begins with careful planning and consideration. Ask yourself first, is this practice right for your lifestyle? Do you have the resources and time to care for a colony?

Steps for success:

  1. Select a site for your hive which provides ample foraging space, sufficient water, and which does not interfere with your surrounding community or neighborhood.
  2. Understand the time comitment and responsibility of looking after your colonies. Expect to reserve anywhere from 1-2 hours per week.
  3. Start small and invest in the right choices. For beginners, Italian bees produce healthy and relatively independent hives. These bees can be purchased from local farmers or online, as well as necessary hives and tools. Start with one-to-two colonies before expanding.
  4. Always wear protective gear such as a veil and a hat, and never disturb a hive without having a smoker near by!

Costs Involved:

  • Bee Hives ($75 - $175)
  • Italian or other bees ($100 - $200)
  • Bee Smoker ($10 - $50)
  • Beekeeping suit ($20 - $100)
  • Electric uncapping knife ($35 - $75)
